Daniel Casabé

Daniel Casabé was born in 1979 in Buenos Aires, Argentina. He attended the Nacional Buenos Aires high school and graduated as a Film Director from the Universidad del Cine (FUC), where he also taught Editing.

He participated in the editing and post-production of feature films by directors such as Mariano Llinás, Alejandro Fadel, Santiago Mitre, Ana Katz and Pablo Trapero. He was part of the editing team of "Tetro" by Francis Ford Coppola, as assistant to Walter Murch.

In 2011 he co-directed his first documentary feature with Edgardo Dieleke: Cracks de nácar, premiered at the XIII BAFICI and nominated for the Cóndor de Plata Award for best documentary of the year.

In 2012 he co-directed La forma exacta de las islas, which premiered at the 27th Mar del Plata International Film Festival in the Latin American Competition, and received the FEISAL Award at the 34th Latin American Film Festival in Havana. In 2014 it was nominated for the Premios Sur as best documentary of the year.

In 2023 he co-directed the fiction feature La Sudestada, based on the award-winning graphic novel by Juan Sáenz Valiente, which premiered in Official Competition at the International Film Festival Rotterdam, then won two official awards at the 24th BAFICI before its theatrical release across Argentina.

Casabé also directed and edited the official Making Of of the series El Eternauta (Netflix, 2025), together with production company OBOL, delivering key promotional content for this landmark production.

"Daniel Casabé is not merely a sensitive editor nor a director with an aesthetic eye. He is a filmmaker with comprehensive technical awareness, capable of intervening at every stage of the filmmaking process with precise, current and effective knowledge. His multiple training (editor, photographer, camera operator, post-producer) allows him to understand a film not as an isolated object, but as an organic system of visual, sonic and technical decisions that dialogue in every frame. In him, craft and poetry coexist, operational speed and narrative depth, technical knowledge and the ethics of time."
